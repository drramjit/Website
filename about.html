About

A sociologist of land, caste, and the long present

Ramjit Kumar is an Assistant Professor of Sociology whose work asks a deceptively simple question: how does an old order survive a new economy?

Born in a district town of north Bihar, I came to sociology through an early and unshakeable curiosity about the village I grew up beside — a place that the textbooks called "traditional" but that seemed, up close, to be in ceaseless negotiation with the wider world. That tension between the apparent fixity of caste and the relentless churn of agrarian economic life has animated everything I have written since.

My approach is resolutely empirical: I spend long stretches in the field, in households and at land records offices, because I believe that grand theory earns its keep only when it survives contact with a real place. Yet I am equally unwilling to abandon theory. The village, for me, is not an escape from the canonical questions of Marx, Weber, and Ambedkar — it is the proving ground on which those questions are most sharply posed.

Commitments

What guides the work

Field before fashion

Theoretical novelty matters less than getting the place right. The field has veto power over the argument.

Caste taken seriously

Not as residue, but as a living institution that adapts, hides, and re-emerges in new economic clothing.

Public sociology

Writing that reaches beyond the seminar room — for policymakers, students, and the communities studied.
