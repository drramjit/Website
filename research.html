Research projects

Fieldwork in progress

Three long-running enquiries, bound by a single thread: how social hierarchy survives, mutates, or dissolves when the rural economy is remade.

Project I · 2024 –

The Tenant's Ledger

When land ownership and land cultivation come apart — the owner in the city, the tiller in the field — what happens to the caste relations historically welded to the soil? Across twelve villages, this project tracks leasing arrangements to show how the separation of title from toil can quietly destabilise hierarchies that looked permanent.

Funding: Indian Council of Social Science Research. Method: household survey (n≈900) plus land-record archives and life-history interviews.

Project II · 2023 –

Migration & the Left-Behind

Circular labour migration drains young men from the village and returns them as remittances. This study follows what happens inside the households that remain — to women's authority, to marriage markets, and to the everyday enforcement of caste boundaries when the people who once policed them are away.

Collaboration: with the Centre for Migration Studies. Method: panel interviews across three migration seasons.

Project III · 2025 –

Caste in the Census

A historical sociology of enumeration: how colonial and post-colonial counting practices did not merely record caste but actively reified it, hardening fluid social groupings into fixed administrative categories whose afterlife shapes politics today.

Type: archival. Sources: census handbooks, gazetteers, and settlement reports, 1881–1991.

Word in the margin Reify — to treat something abstract (like a social category) as if it were a concrete, fixed thing. Enumeration can reify caste: a bureaucratic list turns a loose grouping into a hard, countable object.
